## Summary

When a user unstakes PROFIT tokens, **all accumulated but unclaimed rewards are forfeited** and recycled back into `pool.pending_rewards`. These forfeited rewards are then distributed to *remaining* stakers at the next `update_cumulative` call. This creates a prisoner's-dilemma dynamic: each staker is individually incentivized to wait for others to unstake first, collecting their forfeitures, before exiting themselves. A sufficiently large staker exiting can produce a significant one-time reward windfall for whoever remains, which itself incentivises further hold-or-exit decisions under uncertainty.

## Analysis

### The Forfeiture Mechanism

In `unstake.rs`, lines 155–168:

```rust
// Update rewards BEFORE balance change (checkpoint pattern)
update_rewards(pool, user)?;

// Forfeit unclaimed rewards to pool (redistributed to remaining stakers)
let rewards_forfeited = user.rewards_earned;
if rewards_forfeited > 0 {
    pool.pending_rewards = pool
        .pending_rewards
        .checked_add(rewards_forfeited)
        .ok_or(StakingError::Overflow)?;
    user.rewards_earned = 0;
}
```

The forfeited amount (`user.rewards_earned`) is added directly to `pool.pending_rewards`. This field is the same bucket that receives fresh tax revenue via `deposit_rewards`. At the next `update_cumulative` call (triggered by the Epoch Program during epoch transition), `add_to_cumulative` divides `pending_rewards` by `total_staked` and folds it into `rewards_per_token_stored` — distributing the forfeiture pro-rata to all currently staked users.

### How Large Forfeitures Amplify Remaining Stakes

Consider a staker holding 99% of `total_staked`. If they accumulated rewards R before unstaking without claiming:

1. They forfeit R into `pool.pending_rewards`.
2. After their unstake, `total_staked` drops to 1% of its prior value (only the dead stake + other stakers remain).
3. At the next `update_cumulative`, `reward_per_token = R * PRECISION / new_total_staked`. Because `new_total_staked` is now tiny, the reward-per-token spike is enormous.
4. The remaining 1% of stakers effectively receive R as a windfall — far more than their proportional share of originally deposited tax revenue.

This is not a protocol bug: the forfeited rewards are genuinely redistributed and the accounting is sound (proptest `forfeiture_bounded_by_deposit` verifies user rewards ≤ original deposit). However, it creates the following game-theoretic dynamics:

### The Game of Chicken Structure

**Equilibrium pressure:** Rational stakers will delay unstaking hoping other stakers exit first and donate their forfeited rewards. Being the last to exit yields the accumulated forfeitures from everyone who left before. Being the first to exit means donating your earned rewards to those who remain.

**Asymmetric case — 99% holder:**
- If the 99% holder exits first: they forfeit all their accumulated rewards to the 1% remaining. The 1%'s reward balance increases dramatically.
- If the 99% holder waits for others to exit first: the protocol has very few other stakers to exit, so forfeitures are minimal. The 99% holder captures no meaningful forfeiture windfall.
- **Net result for large holders:** The game-of-chicken dynamic provides little benefit to the dominant holder. Their incentive is simply the claim-and-hold pattern.

**Asymmetric case — small stakers in a large-holder environment:**
- Small stakers holding, say, 0.1% of total stake are rational to wait for the large holder to exit unexpectedly (e.g., due to protocol loss of confidence), then claim the windfall before unstaking.
- This creates rational "reward-vulture" behaviour: small stakers who never intend to hold long-term but stake briefly to capture potential large-holder forfeitures.

### The Claim-then-Unstake Mitigation

The 12-hour `COOLDOWN_SECONDS` gate between `claim` and `unstake` partially neutralises the game: a staker cannot observe another's forfeiture and immediately claim + exit. However:

- The cooldown only applies if `last_claim_ts > 0` (i.e., the user has ever claimed). A user who *never* claims avoids the cooldown entirely on their first unstake.
- After forfeited rewards are added to `pending_rewards`, they do not become accessible to individual users until `update_cumulative` is called — so there is a natural delay anyway between forfeiture and availability.
- The cooldown does not prevent a user from *staking fresh* after a forfeiture event and then claiming when the windfall is distributed.

### Severity Assessment — Why Medium, Not High

This finding is Tier 2 (Medium) rather than High for three reasons:

1. **No direct fund extraction:** The forfeiture mechanism cannot be used to steal funds from the escrow vault. The SOL remains in the protocol and is distributed to real stakers. There is no path to extract more than was deposited (proptest-verified).
2. **The 99% holder cannot self-exploit:** The dominant holder cannot gain by forfeiting their own rewards. They would simply lose yield they had earned. The mechanism is harmful to exiting stakers, not to the protocol or remaining stakers.
3. **Analogous to established patterns:** This is a known trade-off in Synthetix-style staking. The forfeiture-on-exit design is intentional — it serves as a strong hold incentive and aligns long-term stakers. The economic model documentation (`09-economic-model.md:263-268`) explicitly acknowledges this pattern.

The primary risk is **user experience and perception**: unsophisticated users may not understand they forfeit earned rewards on unstake, leading to dissatisfaction or loss of trust. The secondary risk is **governance manipulation**: a large holder could threaten to unstake (and forfeit a large reward pool to remaining stakers) as a coordination mechanism — though this is a very indirect attack vector.

```rust
    }
    // ...balance updates...

```

**`math.rs:111–147`** (`add_to_cumulative`) — Pending rewards including forfeitures are distributed at epoch transition:
```rust
let reward_per_token = (pool.pending_rewards as u128)
    .checked_mul(PRECISION)
    .ok_or(StakingError::Overflow)?
    .checked_div(pool.total_staked as u128)
    .ok_or(StakingError::DivisionByZero)?;

pool.rewards_per_token_stored = pool.rewards_per_token_stored
    .checked_add(reward_per_token)
    .ok_or(StakingError::Overflow)?;
pool.pending_rewards = 0;
```

The `pending_rewards` bucket does not distinguish between fresh tax revenue and forfeited rewards. Both are treated identically in the distribution formula.

**`unstake.rs:125–134`** — Cooldown gate (partially mitigates exploitation):
```rust
if ctx.accounts.user_stake.last_claim_ts > 0 {
    let elapsed = clock
        .unix_timestamp
        .checked_sub(ctx.accounts.user_stake.last_claim_ts)
        .unwrap_or(0);
    require!(elapsed >= COOLDOWN_SECONDS, StakingError::CooldownActive);
}
```

Note: cooldown is skipped when `last_claim_ts == 0` (user has never claimed).

## Economic Assessment

### Scenario 1: Panic Exit (95% stake mass exits simultaneously)

- 95% of stakers forfeit accumulated rewards totalling, say, 10 SOL collectively.
- Remaining 5% of `total_staked` (plus dead stake) receives the 10 SOL windfall at next epoch.
- For a staker holding 1% of original total (now 20% of remaining total): they receive 20% × 10 SOL = 2 SOL extra on top of their normal yield.
- **Assessment:** Windfall is real but not destabilising. The escrow is not drained; the SOL was already there. This is a wealth transfer from exiting stakers to remaining stakers.

### Scenario 2: Strategic "Forfeiture Farming"

An attacker stakes a tiny amount (e.g., 1000 PROFIT above MINIMUM_STAKE), watches on-chain events for large unstake transactions (observable via the `Unstaked` event with `rewards_forfeited` field), then waits for `update_cumulative`, claims the windfall, and exits.

- **Cooldown friction:** They must have claimed at least once before (to start the cooldown timer) — but on a fresh stake, `last_claim_ts == 0`, so the first unstake has no cooldown.
- **Capital efficiency:** 1000 PROFIT staked in a 20M PROFIT pool captures 0.005% of forfeiture. For this to be profitable, the attacker needs to absorb > their staking opportunity cost + transaction fees.
- **Assessment:** Not economically meaningful at protocol scale. The attack requires either a large stake (which has its own opportunity cost) or depends on massive forfeitures (which require others to exit).

### Scenario 3: Coordinated Exit Timing (most realistic concern)

In a bear market or protocol crisis, stakers race to claim before exiting. Whoever claims just before unstaking preserves their earned yield. Whoever panics and unstakes without claiming first loses their earned yield to the pool. This creates a coordination game that amplifies exit pressure: stakers who hear rumours of mass unstaking must decide whether to claim first (starting the 12h cooldown and delaying their exit window) or exit immediately without claiming.

- **Assessment:** This is the most psychologically realistic concern. Under extreme conditions, it can slow orderly exits. However, the cooldown window (12h) is short enough that most users who actively monitor the protocol can claim before unstaking.

## Verdict

**Confirmed — Intended Design with Bounded Exploitability (Tier 2 Medium)**

The forfeiture-on-unstake mechanism is working as designed. The game-of-chicken dynamic is an acknowledged economic trade-off, explicitly documented in the project's economic model. The mechanism cannot be directly exploited to extract funds from the protocol beyond what a staker has legitimately earned. The primary risk is user-experience harm (unaware users losing earned rewards) and a mild coordination-game pressure during crisis exits.

The finding is rated Tier 2 rather than Tier 1 because:
- No direct fund extraction is possible
- The dominant holder (99%) cannot self-exploit — the mechanism harms exiting users, not remaining ones
- Proptest coverage verifies the accounting invariants hold under this pattern
- The claim instruction provides a clean exit path that preserves rewards (the forfeiture only occurs if the user skips claiming before unstaking)

## Recommendations

### R1 — UI Warning (Priority: High, Effort: Low)
The frontend MUST display a prominent warning when a user initiates unstake without first claiming pending rewards. Show the exact lamport amount they will forfeit. This is the single highest-ROI mitigation: the contract design is correct, but users who don't understand it will lose real SOL.

### R2 — Consider `claim_and_unstake` Compound Instruction (Priority: Medium, Effort: Medium)
Implement an atomic `claim_and_unstake` instruction that calls `update_rewards`, transfers SOL rewards, then processes the unstake — all in one transaction. This eliminates the scenario where a user accidentally forfeits rewards by not knowing to claim first. The cooldown gate would need to be re-evaluated for this instruction (it would need to be bypassed or the cooldown enforced differently).

### R3 — Emit Forfeiture Warning in Simulation (Priority: Medium, Effort: Low)
The `Unstaked` event already includes `rewards_forfeited`. Client-side code should simulate the transaction and surface this value before the user signs. Many wallets support TX simulation — integrating this into the dApp flow would prevent uninformed forfeitures.

### R4 — Document the Incentive in Protocol Documentation (Priority: Low, Effort: Low)
The staking documentation should explicitly state: "Unstaking before claiming forfeits all unclaimed rewards to the pool. Always claim your rewards before unstaking." This is a user-education requirement, not a protocol fix.

### R5 — Evaluate Partial-Claim-on-Exit (Priority: Low, Effort: High)
Architecturally, the protocol could be redesigned so that unstake automatically claims pending rewards before processing the exit (removing the forfeiture entirely). This would change the incentive model significantly — the current forfeiture is an intentional alignment mechanism. Before implementing, the team should explicitly decide whether they want the hold incentive or prefer frictionless exits. This is a product decision, not a security fix.
