# H014: Carnage Suppression as Economic Manipulation

**Status:** CONFIRMED
**Severity:** HIGH
**Category:** Economic, MEV
**Origin:** Novel

---

## Summary

An actor with a financial interest in suppressing Carnage buy pressure can permanently prevent the Carnage Fund from ever executing by front-running `consume_randomness` with a version that omits the optional `carnage_state` account. The same root cause as H009 is exploited here, but the motivation is purely economic: maintaining an artificial price divergence between CRIME and FRAUD that benefits the actor's existing position in the "expensive" token.

While H009 characterised this as a griefing/access-control finding, H014 treats the economic dimension as a standalone concern. The two are complementary: H009 proves the mechanism; H014 quantifies the financial incentive and demonstrates that the attack is rational and self-funding for any sufficiently large holder of the expensive token.

---

## Root Cause (shared with H009)

`consume_randomness.rs:76-80` declares `carnage_state` as `Option<Account<'info, CarnageFundState>>`. The Carnage trigger check (step 9, lines 279-328) is guarded by `if let Some(ref carnage_state) = ctx.accounts.carnage_state`. When the account is omitted the handler logs a message and returns `Ok(())`. The VRF has been fully consumed and epoch taxes committed unconditionally before this point (steps 1-8). The check cannot be replayed for the same VRF result.

There is no on-chain mechanism to detect suppression, no penalty for the actor, and no way for the honest crank to recover a Carnage roll that was consumed without the account present.

## Economic Impact Analysis

### 1. The Asymmetric Tax Regime Creates a Sustained Divergence Incentive

Each epoch, VRF byte 0 determines which token is "cheap" (75% flip probability). The cheap token receives:
- **Low buy tax** (1–4%, from its own independent magnitude roll)
- **High sell tax** (11–14%, from its own independent magnitude roll)

The expensive token receives the mirror assignment: high buy tax (11–14%) and low sell tax (1–4%).

This creates a persistent, VRF-governed price asymmetry. Rational traders buy the cheap token (low friction) and sell the expensive token (low friction), which naturally pushes prices toward parity. Carnage accelerates this equalization: when triggered it buys the cheap token, adding concentrated buy pressure at precisely the moment when the token is most discounted.

An actor holding a large position in the **expensive** token is directly harmed by this equalization mechanism. Their position is worth more when price divergence is maintained. They therefore have an active financial incentive to prevent Carnage from firing.

### 2. Suppression Profitability Calculation

Let the following variables represent a realistic mainnet scenario:

| Variable | Value | Source |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Carnage Fund vault accumulation rate | 24% of all swap taxes | `tax_math.rs:95-107` |
| Expected swap volume per epoch | V SOL | Protocol-dependent |
| Tax rate (average) | ~8.5% (midpoint of 3–14%) | `tax_derivation.rs` |
| Carnage vault receives per epoch | 0.24 × 0.085 × V = 0.0204V SOL | Derived |
| Trigger probability per epoch | 11/256 ≈ 4.3% | `carnage.rs:26` |
| Expected Carnage buy size per trigger | min(vault_balance, MAX_CAP) SOL | `carnage_execution.rs:265` |
| MAX_CARNAGE_SWAP_LAMPORTS cap | 1,000,000 SOL (effectively unbounded in practice) | `constants.rs:151` |
| Cost to suppress one epoch | ~0.000005 SOL (1 transaction fee) | Solana baseline |

For a protocol with V = 1,000 SOL per epoch of swap volume (modest but realistic for a launched memecoin):
- Carnage vault receives: 0.0204 × 1,000 = **20.4 SOL per epoch**
- Expected Carnage buy value per epoch: 4.3% × vault_balance (vault grows with each passing epoch)
- After 24 epochs (one day) without triggering, vault holds ~490 SOL
- After 48 epochs without triggering, vault holds ~980 SOL
- Suppression cost for 24 epochs: 24 × 0.000005 SOL = **0.00012 SOL**

**The economic leverage is enormous.** For sub-0.001 SOL total expenditure across a 24-hour suppression campaign, an attacker prevents approximately 4.3% × 490 SOL = **21 SOL** of expected buy pressure against the cheap token — on a single day. The cost/benefit ratio is approximately 175,000:1.

### 3. Position Value Impact

Consider an actor holding X SOL-equivalent worth of the expensive token during a sustained price divergence episode:

- Without Carnage: the cheap token trades at a discount (low buy tax pulls buyers in; high sell tax keeps sellers out). Market forces move slowly.
- With Carnage active: ~2 buy events per day (48 epochs × 4.3%) each deploying concentrated vault SOL. A single 100 SOL Carnage buy in a pool with 500 SOL of SOL reserves creates roughly a 17% price impact on the cheap token (constant-product formula: `new_price = reserve_sol / (reserve_token - tokens_out)`).

For an actor holding 1,000 SOL-equivalent of the expensive token and maintaining divergence over a month:
- Monthly suppression cost: 48 × 30 × 0.000005 SOL ≈ **0.0072 SOL**
- Monthly Carnage buy pressure suppressed: ~0.043 × 48 × 30 × vault_avg_balance = several thousand SOL equivalent in buy pressure depending on volume
- Even if the position only gains 1% from the sustained divergence: **10 SOL profit vs 0.007 SOL cost**

The attack is economically rational at essentially any position size above a few lamports.

### 4. Interaction with Tax Rate Asymmetry

The asymmetric tax regime amplifies the suppression attack:

- Each epoch the cheap side is defined by VRF byte 0 (75% flip probability).
- When CRIME is cheap: buy tax = 1–4%, sell tax = 11–14%. Rational actors flood into CRIME buys.
- Carnage (if unsuppressed) fires into CRIME, amplifying the buy wave.
- **An actor short CRIME** (or holding a large FRAUD position during a CRIME-cheap epoch) benefits from suppressing Carnage: no concentrated buy pressure allows CRIME to stay discounted longer.

Because tax rates flip each epoch, the actor needs to run the suppression bot every single epoch to cover both directions. But at ~0.000005 SOL per epoch, this is still essentially free.

The critical insight is that the attacker doesn't need to predict when Carnage would fire — suppressing it unconditionally (every epoch) has zero marginal cost and guaranteed benefit. The tax asymmetry creates the market condition; suppression maintains it indefinitely.

### 5. Carnage Fund Vault Accumulation Under Suppression

Each suppressed epoch, the vault continues receiving 24% of swap taxes. Under continuous suppression:

- The vault grows monotonically, never discharging.
- This SOL is economically "trapped" — it sits idle instead of providing buy pressure.
- At high protocol volumes, this could represent hundreds or thousands of SOL permanently locked in the vault (until the bug is fixed and the next trigger fires, at which point the vault still only deploys `min(balance, MAX_CAP)` — which at 1,000,000 SOL cap is effectively the full balance).
- **The trapped SOL is a secondary harm to stakers**: the 71% staking allocation still distributes normally, but the 24% Carnage allocation accumulates without ever performing its designed function (reinforcing buy pressure and reducing sell pressure on staked token values).

## Attack Profitability Assessment

### Scenario A: Small Holder Griefing (No Position)
- Motivation: Competitor protocol, short-seller wanting death spiral
- Cost per month: ~0.007 SOL
- Impact: Protocol's core differentiating mechanic never fires; community trust erodes
- Verdict: **Extremely low cost, high narrative damage**

### Scenario B: Large Holder of Expensive Token
- Motivation: Direct position protection
- Example: Hold 10,000 SOL-equivalent of FRAUD during CRIME-cheap epoch
- Suppression cost per month: ~0.007 SOL
- Position benefit: Potentially hundreds of SOL per month from maintained divergence
- Cost/benefit ratio: ~10,000–100,000:1
- Verdict: **Dominantly rational attack**

### Scenario C: Coordinated MEV Bot (Multiple Attackers)
- If two actors simultaneously run suppression, the first one to land wins for that epoch
- This is a Solana leader-based race, not an EVM mempool auction
- An actor with reliable stake-weighted validator access can guarantee consistent suppression
- Verdict: **Accessible to sophisticated MEV operators**

### Attack Lifecycle
1. Deploy bot monitoring `EpochState.vrf_pending` via websocket subscription to epoch program
2. When `vrf_pending == true` and randomness account reveals, immediately submit `consume_randomness` with `carnage_state = None`
3. With standard priority fee the attacker wins the race whenever the legitimate crank has similar or lower priority
4. Every suppressed epoch costs ~0.000005 SOL in fees; the honest crank's TX is rejected with `NoVrfPending` (already consumed)
5. Repeat every 30 minutes indefinitely

**No positions, staking, or on-chain history required.** Any funded wallet suffices.

## Verdict

**CONFIRMED — HIGH severity.**

This finding is independently HIGH-severity even after accounting for H009, because the economic incentive structure makes this attack *rational and self-funding* for any participant with a meaningful position. The combination of:

1. Near-zero attack cost (~0.000005 SOL per epoch)
2. Enormous economic leverage (thousands-to-one cost/benefit in realistic scenarios)
3. Complete undetectability on-chain (legitimate-looking `consume_randomness` succeeds)
4. Irreversibility of each suppressed epoch (VRF is spent)
5. Cumulative vault lock-up damaging the protocol's economic design

makes this one of the highest-impact economic vulnerabilities in the codebase. The severity is HIGH rather than CRITICAL because (a) vault funds are not directly stolen — the SOL remains locked and would deploy correctly once the bug is fixed, and (b) the attack harms the protocol economically without enabling direct unauthorized fund access.

The severity would be upgraded to **CRITICAL** if combined with any mechanism to also drain the vault, or if a future instruction were added that allows the vault accumulation to be redirected.

## Recommendations

### Fix (Required — same as H009)

Make `carnage_state` mandatory. Change the account declaration in `consume_randomness.rs:76-80` from `Option<Account<'info, CarnageFundState>>` to `Account<'info, CarnageFundState>`:

```rust
// AFTER FIX:
#[account(
    seeds = [CARNAGE_FUND_SEED],
    bump,
)]
pub carnage_state: Account<'info, CarnageFundState>,
```

This single-line change eliminates the entire attack surface. The "backward compatibility" rationale in the comment is inapplicable once the protocol is deployed to mainnet with `initialize_carnage_fund` called.

### Defense in Depth (Recommended)

1. **Emit a distinguishable event** when Carnage would have triggered but the account was absent. This provides off-chain monitoring capability even before a program upgrade.

2. **Monitoring alert**: Protocol operators should subscribe to on-chain events and alert if `CarnagePending` events stop appearing at the expected ~4.3% frequency over any rolling 48-epoch window. Sustained zero-fire rate is a strong signal of active suppression.

3. **Slippage floor for vault growth**: Consider a mechanism that, after N epochs without a Carnage trigger (even non-triggering epochs), flags the state for review. This is a belt-and-suspenders governance check, not a protocol fix.

### Notes on Fix Priority

H009 and H014 share the same one-line fix. Fixing H009 simultaneously resolves H014's attack vector. Given the rational economic incentive demonstrated here, this fix should be treated as **blocking for mainnet deployment**.
