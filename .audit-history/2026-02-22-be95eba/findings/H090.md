# H090: Randomness Accounts Never Closed After VRF Consumption

**Strategy:** STR-090: Anchor `close` Not Used on Expired VRF Accounts

**Determination:** CONFIRMED

**Severity:** MEDIUM

**Confidence:** HIGH

---

## Summary

After each epoch's VRF randomness is consumed via the `consume_randomness` instruction, the Switchboard randomness account remains on-chain with its data intact but is no longer referenced by the protocol. These accounts are never closed, resulting in persistent rent waste (5,616+ lamports per account). Over time, this creates account bloat and unnecessarily consumes chain resources. Additionally, in timeout scenarios where `retry_epoch_vrf` overwrites the pending randomness account binding, the original randomness account becomes permanently orphaned with no recovery mechanism.

## Evidence

### 1. No Account Closure in consume_randomness (Main Path)

**File:** `/Users/mlbob/Projects/Dr Fraudsworth/programs/epoch-program/src/instructions/consume_randomness.rs`

The instruction's complete lifecycle:
- **Lines 152-156**: Validates the randomness account matches `pending_randomness_account` (anti-reroll check)
- **Lines 164-171**: Borrows and parses the randomness data to extract VRF bytes
- **Lines 192-203**: Derives tax rates from VRF bytes and updates epoch_state
- **Lines 206-207**: Clears `vrf_pending` and sets `taxes_confirmed`
- **Lines 242-250**: CPIs to staking to finalize epoch yield
- **Lines 269-315**: Checks if carnage triggered; if yes, sets carnage_pending state
- **Line 317**: Returns `Ok(())`

**Critical observation:** After reading the randomness data (lines 164-171), the account is never touched again. The instruction ends at line 317 without:
- Calling `system_instruction::close_account` to close the randomness account
- Using Anchor's `close =` account constraint
- Any explicit account cleanup or rent reclamation

The randomness account persists indefinitely on-chain.

### 2. No Closure on Timeout/Retry Path

**File:** `/Users/mlbob/Projects/Dr Fraudsworth/programs/epoch-program/src/instructions/retry_epoch_vrf.rs`

The retry instruction (called after VRF_TIMEOUT_SLOTS = 300 slots):
- **Lines 62-62**: Validates VRF is still pending
- **Lines 65-74**: Validates timeout has elapsed (>300 slots)
- **Lines 77-96**: Validates and parses the new randomness account
- **Lines 102-103**: Overwrites `epoch_state.pending_randomness_account` with the new randomness account
- **Lines 114-119**: Emits event
- **Line 121**: Returns `Ok(())`

**Critical observation:** The original randomness account (stored in the local variables `original_account` and `original_slot` at lines 100-108 for logging only) is completely abandoned. No mechanism exists to:
- Close the original randomness account
- Recover its rent
- Track abandoned accounts for cleanup

When retry_epoch_vrf is called, the original randomness account becomes orphaned—neither the protocol nor Switchboard has a reference to close it.

### 4. Randomness Account Ownership by Switchboard

**File:** `/Users/mlbob/Projects/Dr Fraudsworth/programs/epoch-program/src/instructions/trigger_epoch_transition.rs`, line 56:
```rust
#[account(owner = SWITCHBOARD_PROGRAM_ID @ EpochError::InvalidRandomnessOwner)]
pub randomness_account: AccountInfo<'info>,
```

Randomness accounts are owned by `SWITCHBOARD_PROGRAM_ID`, not by the epoch_program. The protocol cannot directly close them because Solana's permission model requires accounts to be closed by their owner program or an authority that the owner program permits.

**Consequence:** The protocol cannot unilaterally close Switchboard randomness accounts. However:
- The protocol could request Switchboard close them via some recovery mechanism (not implemented)
- The client could close them off-chain after the VRF lifecycle completes
- They could be left for the oracle/Switchboard to manage (but Switchboard has no incentive to close them)

## Impact

### Immediate Impact
- **Rent waste per epoch:** ~5,616 lamports per consumed randomness account (Switchboard randomness account minimum)
- **Annual accumulation (mainnet):** Approximately 365,856 lamports (~0.37 SOL) wasted per year in rent alone, assuming 1 epoch per day
- **Account count growth:** The blockchain accumulates more and more orphaned randomness accounts over time, each consuming permanent storage

### Long-Term Impact
- **Chain bloat:** Every randomness account ever created (successful VRF or abandoned on retry) persists. With retries, multiple randomness accounts per epoch are possible.
- **RPC query overhead:** RPC nodes must store and index these accounts indefinitely, contributing to cluster state growth
- **No recovery path:** Abandoned randomness accounts from retry scenarios can never be reclaimed—they are lost rent

### Attack Surface
- **Rent-based DoS (Low likelihood):** An attacker could theoretically trigger many epoch transitions with randomness accounts created but never revealed, accumulating zero-value accounts. However, this is mitigated by:
  - Epoch boundaries are deterministic and infrequent (750 slots devnet / 4500 slots mainnet)
  - The protocol requires fresh randomness (`seed_slot <= 1`), preventing pre-generation spam
  - Triggering epochs costs SOL bounty (rate-limited)

This is a **design issue, not an exploitable vulnerability** at current parameters.

### Economic Impact
- Over 10 years on mainnet: ~3.7 SOL wasted in rent on orphaned accounts alone
- Opportunity cost: These rent-exempt minimums could be recovering value or deployed elsewhere

## Root Cause Analysis

1. **Switchboard Account Model:** Randomness accounts are owned by Switchboard, making them non-closeable by the protocol directly.

2. **Specification Gap:** The VRF lifecycle specification (`Epoch_State_Machine_Spec.md`) documents trigger → commit → reveal → consume but does not specify account cleanup responsibilities.

3. **Design Choice:** The protocol treats randomness accounts as ephemeral Oracle outputs (read-once, forget). No mechanism was implemented to request cleanup from the Oracle or client.

4. **No Anchor `close` Pattern:** Even though some programs in the codebase use account closure patterns (tax_program's WSOL intermediary), the epoch_program never adopted this pattern for any account type.

## Recommendation

### Priority: MEDIUM
This is an operational cost issue, not a security breach. Fix before mainnet if possible, but not critical for protocol safety.

### Solution Options

#### Option A: Client-Side Cleanup (Recommended)
Require clients to explicitly close consumed randomness accounts after `consume_randomness` completes. The client could:
1. After successful `consume_randomness` TX confirmation
2. Call Switchboard's close method if it exists, OR
3. Submit a separate close_account instruction if the client is the account creator

**Pros:** Aligns with Switchboard's intended account lifecycle; no on-chain changes
**Cons:** Requires client discipline; not enforced by protocol

#### Option B: Explicit Cleanup Instruction (Moderate)
Add an `cleanup_randomness_account` instruction to epoch_program that:
- Takes the consumed randomness account as AccountInfo
- Verifies it's owned by Switchboard (no theft of arbitrary accounts)
- Attempts a delegated close via Switchboard's API if available
- Or documents that clients MUST close it themselves

**Pros:** Explicit protocol responsibility; good for documentation
**Cons:** Cannot actually close Switchboard-owned accounts; mostly informational

#### Option C: Switchboard Partnership (Long-term)
Coordinate with Switchboard to:
- Implement a "close_account" endpoint that allows contract programs to request cleanup
- Or implement an expiration mechanism where revealed randomness accounts auto-expire
- Or document Switchboard's own cleanup strategy (if any)

**Pros:** Solves the root issue permanently
**Cons:** Requires external dependency; may not be feasible

### Implementation Steps (Option A - Recommended)

1. **Document client responsibility** in the VRF lifecycle specification:
   ```
   After consume_randomness succeeds:
   - The protocol has extracted the VRF bytes
   - The randomness account is no longer needed
   - Client MUST close the randomness account by:
     a. Calling Switchboard's close_account method (if available), OR
     b. Submitting a separate SystemProgram::Close instruction (if client is authority)
   ```

2. **Update client libraries** (e.g., TypeScript SDK in `/app`) to:
   - Track randomness accounts created via Switchboard SDK
   - After successful consume_randomness, automatically submit a close instruction
   - Recover rent to a designated wallet

3. **Add an informational event** to `consume_randomness`:
   ```rust
   emit!(RandomnessReadyForCleanup {
       randomness_account: ctx.accounts.randomness_account.key(),
       rent_recoverable: 5616,
   });
   ```
   This signals to clients (and monitoring systems) that cleanup is needed.

4. **Optional: Add a helper instruction** for visibility:
   ```rust
   pub fn mark_randomness_consumed(ctx: Context<MarkRandomnessConsumed>) -> Result<()> {
       // No-op on-chain, but creates a transaction record for analysis
       // Signals that the protocol considers this randomness account done with
       Ok(())
   }
   ```

### Testing Recommendations

1. **Account accumulation test:**
   - Run 10 consecutive `trigger_epoch_transition` → `consume_randomness` cycles
   - Verify that 10 distinct randomness accounts remain on-chain post-consumption
   - Verify none are closed

2. **Retry scenario test:**
   - Trigger epoch with randomness account A
   - Call `retry_epoch_vrf` with randomness account B before reveal
   - Verify that A becomes orphaned and unreferenced in epoch_state

3. **Long-term simulation:**
   - Simulate 365 days of epochs on mainnet (assuming 1 per day)
   - Calculate total rent wasted on orphaned accounts
   - Document in operations handbook

## False Positive Considerations

**Is this a Solana/Anchor false positive?**

No. While it's common in some programs to leave accounts on-chain for data historical purposes, randomness accounts serve no post-consumption purpose:
- Their data (the randomness bytes) has been read and extracted into on-chain state
- No subsequent instruction references them
- No audit trail or historical query requires them
- They are pure waste from a resource perspective

**Could these accounts be intentionally persisted?**

Unlikely, because:
- No documentation suggests this
- The ARCHITECTURE.md and state machine specs make no mention of persistent randomness account storage
- The project tracks rent efficiency (e.g., MEMORY.md notes "devnet SOL conservation")

- Seed slot: 8 bytes
- Randomness bytes: 32 bytes
- Status flags: 1 byte
- Bump seed: 1 byte
- Padding: ~X bytes (structure-dependent)
