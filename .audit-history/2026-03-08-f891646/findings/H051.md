# H051 -- Bonding Curve Purchase Price Manipulation

**File:** `programs/bonding_curve/src/instructions/purchase.rs`
**Supporting:** `programs/bonding_curve/src/math.rs`, `programs/bonding_curve/src/constants.rs`
**Severity:** INFORMATIONAL (defenses adequate)
**Status:** No vulnerability found -- sandwich protection is present and effective

---

## Hypothesis

Linear bonding curve price = a * tokens_sold + b. A large purchase moves the price along the curve. Check if purchase has `minimum_tokens_out`. Verify sandwich protection on bonding curve.

## Evidence

### 1. Slippage Protection (`minimum_tokens_out`) -- PRESENT

The `purchase` instruction accepts a user-supplied `minimum_tokens_out` parameter (line 100) and enforces it at Step 9 (line 178-181):

```rust
pub fn handler<'info>(
    ctx: Context<'_, '_, 'info, 'info, Purchase<'info>>,
    sol_amount: u64,
    minimum_tokens_out: u64,       // <-- slippage parameter
) -> Result<()> {
    // ...
    // Step 9: Slippage protection
    require!(
        actual_tokens >= minimum_tokens_out,
        CurveError::SlippageExceeded
    );
```

This check fires AFTER the partial-fill adjustment (Step 6-7), meaning it compares the actual tokens the user will receive against their minimum expectation. The sell instruction (`sell.rs:114,191`) has an equivalent `minimum_sol_out` parameter.

Integration tests confirm enforcement: `lifecycle.test.ts:762-792` tests that an absurdly high `minimum_tokens_out` triggers `SlippageExceeded`.

### 2. Sandwich Attack Surface Analysis

**On-chain ordering matters.** On Solana, a sandwich attacker would need to:
1. Front-run: Purchase tokens to advance `tokens_sold`, raising the price.
2. Victim TX: The victim buys at a higher price, getting fewer tokens.
3. Back-run: Sell tokens at the now-higher price position.

**Defenses that mitigate this:**

| Defense | Location | Effect |
|---------|----------|--------|
| `minimum_tokens_out` | purchase.rs:178-181 | Victim rejects if output below expectation |
| Per-wallet cap (20M tokens) | purchase.rs:135-141, constants.rs:49 | Limits attacker's single-wallet front-run size to 20M tokens (~4.3% of supply) |
| 15% sell tax on back-run | sell.rs:174-179 | Attacker pays 15% ceil-rounded tax on their back-run sell, making sandwich unprofitable |
| Minimum purchase (0.05 SOL) | purchase.rs:116-119 | Prevents micro-buy spam |
| Linear curve (not constant-product) | math.rs:56-110 | Price impact is deterministic from `tokens_sold` -- no reserve manipulation |

**The 15% sell tax is the key defense.** In a sandwich attack on a bonding curve:
- The attacker buys X tokens at price P1 (paying integral from `tokens_sold` to `tokens_sold + X`).
- Victim buys at a higher price.
- Attacker sells X tokens back, receiving 85% of the integral value (15% goes to tax escrow).

For the sandwich to be profitable, the price increase caused by the victim's purchase must exceed 15% of the attacker's position value. Given the linear price curve spans 900 to 3450 lamports/token over 460M tokens, the price movement per token is (3450-900)/460M = ~0.00000554 lamports/token. Even a maximum front-run of 20M tokens only moves the price by ~0.111 lamports/token, which is ~0.0032% of the starting price. The 15% sell tax dwarfs any extractable price movement.

### 3. Partial Fill Handling

When the curve is nearly full, a purchase may be partially filled (Step 6-7, lines 146-162). The SOL cost is recalculated via `calculate_sol_for_tokens` using ceil rounding (protocol-favored), and the user's excess SOL is never charged (only `actual_sol` is transferred). The slippage check at Step 9 applies to `actual_tokens`, correctly protecting the user against unexpected partial fills.

### 4. Price Determinism

Unlike AMM pools where reserves can be manipulated, the bonding curve price is a pure function of `tokens_sold` (a single u64 counter on the CurveState PDA). There is no external oracle or pool reserve that can be independently manipulated. The only way to change the price is to execute a purchase or sell, both of which have economic costs (15% sell tax, minimum purchase amounts).

## Verdict

**No vulnerability.** The bonding curve purchase instruction has adequate sandwich protection:

1. **`minimum_tokens_out`** is present and enforced, allowing users to reject unfavorable fills.
2. **15% sell tax** makes sandwich attacks economically unviable -- the tax cost far exceeds any extractable price movement on a linear curve.
3. **Per-wallet cap** limits front-run size.
4. **Deterministic pricing** from a single state variable eliminates reserve-manipulation attacks.

The combination of user-facing slippage protection and protocol-level economic disincentives (15% tax) provides robust defense against price manipulation on the bonding curve.

## Severity Justification

INFORMATIONAL -- all expected protections are in place and working correctly. The 15% sell tax provides a uniquely strong economic barrier against sandwich attacks that most protocols lack.
