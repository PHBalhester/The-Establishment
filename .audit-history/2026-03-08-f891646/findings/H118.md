# H118 -- Bonding Curve: Refund After Partial Sales (Tax Escrow Impact)

## Status: NOT A BUG -- Correctly Handled via Escrow Consolidation

## Target
`programs/bonding_curve/src/instructions/claim_refund.rs`

## Hypothesis
If a bonding curve fails after buy/sell cycles, the refund pool may differ from what users expect because sell taxes (15%) were extracted from the SOL vault and routed to the tax escrow. Users who sold received less SOL (net of tax), and the tax is sitting in a separate account. Does this cause unfair refund distribution?

## Analysis

### The Tax Leakage Problem

During the Active phase:
- **Buys** deposit SOL into `sol_vault`. `sol_raised` tracks cumulative gross inflows.
- **Sells** withdraw SOL from `sol_vault` in two pieces:
  - 85% (`sol_net`) goes to the seller.
  - 15% (`tax`) goes to `tax_escrow` (a separate PDA).
- `sol_returned` tracks the GROSS sell amount (before tax split).

After buy/sell cycles, the sol_vault balance is:
```
sol_vault = sol_raised - sol_returned + rent_exempt
```

But the actual SOL available for refunds should include the tax escrow, because those taxes came from seller proceeds and represent value that should be returned to remaining token holders.

### The Consolidation Solution

The protocol handles this with a two-step process:

1. **`consolidate_for_refund`** (must be called before any `claim_refund`):
   - Moves all available SOL from `tax_escrow` into `sol_vault` (minus rent-exempt minimum).
   - Sets `escrow_consolidated = true` on CurveState.

2. **`claim_refund`** requires `escrow_consolidated == true`:
   - Computes `refund_pool = sol_vault.lamports() - rent_exempt`.
   - This now includes both the remaining buy SOL AND the accumulated sell taxes.
   - Proportional distribution: `floor(user_balance * refund_pool / tokens_sold)`.

### Is the Refund Pool Fair?

After consolidation, the refund pool contains:
```
refund_pool = (sol_raised - sol_net_returned_to_sellers - tax_sent_to_escrow) + tax_consolidated_back
            = sol_raised - sol_net_returned_to_sellers
```

This means:
- Sellers already received their SOL (minus 15% tax). They hold fewer tokens (returned to vault).
- The 15% tax is returned to the refund pool, benefiting remaining holders.
- Remaining holders get a proportional share of the full pool.

**This is correct and fair.** The bonding curve's integral math ensures that the vault always holds at least the integral value of outstanding tokens (the solvency assertion in `sell.rs` enforces this). The tax consolidation adds EXTRA SOL back, meaning refund claimers actually receive slightly MORE per token than the bare integral value.

### Edge Cases

**All tokens sold, no sells occurred:** Tax escrow has 0 (only rent-exempt). Consolidation moves 0. Refund pool = full sol_raised. Correct.

**Heavy sell activity:** Many sells extracted SOL (net) and tax. Consolidation returns the tax portion. Remaining holders share the pool. Floor rounding favors the protocol (last claimer gets any dust). The `tokens_sold` denominator shrinks after each claim (step 9 in claim_refund), ensuring later claimers compute their share against the reduced pool and reduced outstanding tokens. This is the standard shrinking-denominator pattern.

**Filled curve with partner Failed:** A Filled curve (460M tokens sold, ~1000 SOL raised) where no one sold has the full ~1000 SOL in the vault. Refund is straightforward. If sells occurred on the Filled curve (sells are only allowed in Active state, not Filled), the tax escrow would capture those taxes. But sells are blocked once the curve reaches Filled status, so for a Filled curve, the only sells happened while Active (before filling). The consolidation still correctly handles this.

### Division Safety

The refund computation uses u128 intermediates:
```rust
let refund_amount = ((user_balance as u128)
    .checked_mul(refund_pool as u128)
    .ok_or(CurveError::Overflow)?
    / (total_outstanding as u128)) as u64;
```

Maximum values: `user_balance` <= 460e12, `refund_pool` <= ~1000e9, product <= ~4.6e23, well within u128 max (~3.4e38). Floor division ensures the pool is never over-drained.

## Verdict

**Refund pool correctly accounts for sell taxes via the consolidation mechanism.** The two-step process (consolidate then claim) ensures all SOL -- including accumulated sell taxes -- is available for proportional refund. The design is sound and the math is safe.

## Severity: Informational
